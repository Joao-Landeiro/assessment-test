A word on this assessment

This is a first version of a diagnostic tool grounded in peer-reviewed research from implementation science, organisational theory, and socio-technical systems. While it is not a substitute for working with an experienced advisor, the underlying model is designed to surface structural patterns that commonly undermine strategy implementation in firms that serve clients with teams.

Dimension 1: Truth

Can people in the client organisation say what they actually think?
Often, it's the unsaid facts that hinder implementation. Implementation is always challenging, but impossible if there's no way to communicate what is going on and what could need adjustment.

1. Do client team members publicly agree with your recommendations, only to express doubts or confusion privately afterwards?

Measures: Pluralistic Ignorance (Westphal & Bednar)

2. If your diagnostic uncovers “uncomfortable truths,” does client leadership scrutinize your methodology to dismiss the bad news instead of engaging with the issues?

Measures: Pathological Information Flow (Westrum)

3. Do clients seem to use your strategy mostly to provide “political cover” while intentionally keeping actual operations unchanged?

Measures: Strategic Misrepresentation / Optimism Bias (Flyvbjerg)

Dimension 2: Coherence

Do different groups share the same understanding of what the strategy means?
It takes time for changes to take hold, and in this period, the client organization will need to stay aligned. This alignment is often elusive and fleeting, requiring frequent reinforcement.

4. When working with clients, do you find that different departments (e.g., Sales vs. Product) interpret your core strategy recommendations in noticeably different ways?

Measures: Excessive Interpretive Flexibility (Star & Griesemer)

5. Do you observe client teams using terminology from your strategy work to defend their own pre-existing goals?

Measures: Boundary Object Friction (Black)

6. Three months after delivery, do different team members give noticeably different answers when asked “why are we doing it this new way”?

Measures: Normalization Coherence Collapse (May & Finch)

Dimension 3: Velocity

Can the organisational structure support the pace and coordination the strategy demands?
Organizations have a different capacity to change. In many cases, the change that is being proposed is beyond what the client organization could be expected to absorb,

7. Does the recommended strategy require collaboration across departments that currently operate on separate reporting lines, budgets, or physical locations?

Measures: Mirroring Mismatch (Colfer & Baldwin)

8. Do you find yourself trying to manage a “fuzzy” strategic shift using linear project management tools (e.g., Gantt charts, rigid milestone trackers)?

Measures: Uncertainty vs. Equivocality Mismatch (Daft & Lengel)

9. Do projects stall because “Decision A cannot be made until Department B and Department C align,” but they never seem to meet?

Measures: Interdependence Overload (Galbraith)

Dimension 4: Material Adoption

Are people actually changing their day-to-day behaviour?

10. When presenting recommendations, do client team members struggle to explain the strategy back in their own words?

Measures: Absorptive Capacity Atrophy (Cohen & Levinthal)

11. After a strategic shift is agreed upon, do the client’s incentive structures, performance reviews, or resource allocations remain unchanged?

Measures: Implementation Climate Deficit (Klein & Sorra)

12. Do the client’s employees continue to execute their old daily workflows and routines, effectively ignoring the new processes defined in your strategy?

Measures: Translation Failure (Callon; Røvik)
